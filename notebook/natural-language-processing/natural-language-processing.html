    Up to the 1980s, most natural language processing systems were based on complex sets of hand-written rules. Starting in the late 1980s, however, there was a revolution in natural language processing with the introduction of machine learning algorithms for language processing. This was due to both the steady increase in computational power and a better understanding of the theories of linguistics (e.g. transformational grammar), whose theoretical underpinnings to the structure discouraged the sort of corpus linguistics that underlies the machine-learning approach to language processing. For example instinct, and the communications involved in acting on instinct, isn't something that is applicable to machine learning.

    This is most likely due to the fact the these two aspects of linguistics, one attempting to describe how humans use the language, the other attempting to act as a more efficient means of retrieving data, while related, aren't attempting to achieve the same goal. One is for human understanding the other is for interfacing with computer systems. How we use words to understand our environment do not apply to how information is stored and retrieved from a database. However, in our attempt to implement our primary common language as a means of information retrieval, we apply the same rules to the structure of the database to make the retrieval process more intuitive. This often leads to confusion when we refer to non-organic objects the way we do other organic objects. Reading a book...written by human who generated sentences based on the linguistic rules. In this analogy, the book is a database. Or a Library as another example, is a hugely complex database of generated language. The difference between and a Library and a Computer Database, is the speed at which you can retrieve information.
