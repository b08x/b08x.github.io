So, personally as someone who wasn't diagnosed until their mid 30's, my life hasn't been one set on a path as much as it's just something I find myself wandering through. In my idealized life, I would use the skills I've learned in music theory and audio engineering to be influential musician who affected changes in society to some degree....So, to explain the order of how I came to have an expertise is a matter of love, loyalty and practical living. My natural tendencies and skill-set are based more in communication and psychology, but I knew I had to continually refine my technical skill-set as my natural tendencies alone don't achieve my desired goal, which is to ensure that myself and my partner are taken care of. In other words, there isn't much you can get from the story my experience besides hope and cautionary insight, as I have much more experience with failure than I do success in terms of a "mainstream" career path. This something that bothered me tremendously for most of my younger adult years until I figured out how to articulate that experience and help people with it. That's one of the reasons I choose to focus on Natural Language Processing now as opposed expanding my knowledge in Development Operations. Now it's much more relevant and is more aligned with my natural tendencies. I still need medication for the highly technical aspects, which is something I'm still looking to reconcile with.

One friend of mine focuses on learning many languages to expand his understanding and is sort of successful with that but currently his passion is firefighting. While I mostly focus on mastering two specific languages; Ruby and Shell with my passion now leaning more towards that of an educator or author. Neil Degrasse Tyson is big influence for me here as I believe what is most needed now, are better explanations for the things we do. So like right now, I'm working on putting together a demo app using Ruby that shows NLP techniques so others can learn, which is different from strictly software engineering where one is focused on the outcome of a product. I use Ruby because, to me, it's looks more like natural language than any other programming language I considered.

You can see from a website that I work on from time to time ( https://b08x.github.io ) that my focus is fairly scattered, which for me, is a result of learning too many things. This is annoying for me, as my addiction to learning new things ended up being a hindrance with age. I really didn't need to learn HTML/CSS to accomplish my goals. It's something to keep in mind with ADHD and the medication.

One funny aspect to me, is the literal software I had to work with when very first starting in this field, has been obsolete for years. In 1997 my focus was on cracking serial numbers for video games and figuring out "Network Neighborhood" in Windows 98. But if I say that without further analysis, it doesn't give the immediate impression that what I was actually learning was a form of reverse engineering and the standard suite of network protocols. I figure these thing out by taking a working example of whatever I'm dealing with, break it apart into it's individual components, then try to replicate the functionality using what I know. So for example, with Python, scour github for an application that looks good to you and try to replicate what you see. Eventually, if you stay in the ML field you will most likely need to pick up skills in the R programming language as well, so the more familiar you become with the general process, as opposed to the rules of the a language, the easier time you'll adapting to whatever changes in the future. These are all things to figure out, over time, in terms of what works best for you. The longer you persist with it, the easier it becomes to reconcile the chaotic nature of information technology.

So, this repo for example: https://github.com/morph-labs
contains several very promising projects mostly written in Python. The problem here is that all of these projects, for me at least, required many years of accumulated repetitive experience just to understand it let alone become involved with it. Which is why I focus on NLP these days hoping I can use my experience, supplemented with the technology, to help explain that without a path set by traditional education, getting to the point mastering these skills requires a lot resilience for people that are labeled such as we are. It seems to me, you are already far ahead of where I was at 22 (at 22 I didn't even know what Linux was) and you're not as depressed as amount of information is most certainly intimidating for people that struggle with ADHD and the resulting depression and can be discouraging. If you hadn't mentioned the fact that you're perusing medication, I wouldn't be sharing all of this with you because I know right now I have sort an advantage.

Here's a video where I recorded myself in the beginning stages of the struggle to learn specific concepts for the purposes of programming an application. If you notice, it's fairly dull and I don't sound very confident, as I sort of just talk outloud to myself while trying to best understand this very specific thing. Mostly what I'm doing here, is just absorbing myself with the information until something clicks. I'm also trying to show a more realistic depiction of the learning process that I don't see much of on youtube. It's meant to show that no matter how experienced a person is, the learning process is a challenge we all struggle with when we not putting a fancy show for each other. What most videos on youtube don't show, is the rather dull process that leads to success, they only show the final outcome, which is misleading.

Don't mind the description in the video, that's just my sense of humor...
