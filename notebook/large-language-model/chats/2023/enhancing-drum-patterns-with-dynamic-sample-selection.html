The article on "Cadence" explains that in Western musical theory, a cadence is "a melodic or harmonic configuration that creates a sense of resolution [finality or pause]." A harmonic cadence is a progression of (at least) two chords that concludes a phrase, section, or piece of music. A rhythmic cadence is a characteristic rhythmic pattern that indicates the end of a phrase.

Cadences are classified by their progression style and function. Authentic cadences progress from the subdominant or the dominant to the tonic. Plagal cadences, also known as the "Amen Cadence" because of its frequent setting to the text "Amen" in hymns, progress from the subdominant to the tonic. Half cadences, also known as imperfect cadences, are any cadences ending on the dominant; the most common is the dominant preceded by the tonic in root position. Deceptive cadences are also known as interrupted cadences, and they are created by any cadence ending on a chord other than the tonic. The most common is a dominant followed by a sixth chord.

Cadences can be either conclusive, giving a sense of finality, or inconclusive, giving a sense of pause. Conclusive cadences are either perfect or imperfect. Perfect cadences are strongest and most final-sounding; in these, the dominant chord is followed by the tonic chord, and both are in root position. In an imperfect cadence, either the dominant or tonic chord is inverted, or a subdominant chord precedes the dominant chord. Inconclusive cadences are either half cadences or interrupted cadences.

This concept could be used to create a sense of resolution or finality in the music piece you're working on. It could also be used to create a sense of pause or to keep the music piece open-ended.
