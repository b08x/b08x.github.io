First person is ==a grammatical person that includes the speaker==. It is a style of writing where a story is told from the perspective of a character. In first-person narration, the narrator is a character in the story who tells the story from their own point of view. The narration usually uses the pronoun "I" or "we" if the narrator is speaking as part of a group.

==Second person refers to the person or people the speaker is addressing==. For example, "I am speaking to you about her". In this sentence, "I" is in the first person and "you" is in the second person.

Third person is ==a narrative style that uses pronouns like "he" or "she" to describe characters' actions==. The narrator is outside the story and doesn't directly address the reader. Third person point of view can be categorized as ==omniscient, limited, or objective==.

- Third-person limited
    
    The narrator knows only the thoughts of the protagonist (the hero of the story). The motives of other characters are shown exclusively through dialogue and action.
    
- Third-person omniscient
    
    The narrator is not a character in the story and can share everything they know. The narrator has no biases and can present the thoughts, feelings, and actions of multiple characters. 
    

Third-person limited is more intimate, but it's also limiting. The reader only knows what the character whose head the author is writing from knows. Omniscient is less intimate and can come off as distant. Typically a third person omniscient voice is a bit more objective, meaning we see more facts and observations rather than thoughts, and third person limited is a bit more subjective, meaning we see more thoughts.

==Yes, you can switch between third person omniscient and limited in a novel, but not within the same scene or chapter==. Some say this is an old-fashioned technique, but it can be an effective way to tell a story with multiple side plots.

The alternating limited omniscient point of view is a literary technique that uses more than one character and shifts between them. It's also known as "head hopping" because the reader moves between different characters' heads.

Limited omniscient point of view (often called a “close third”) is **when an author sticks closely to one character but remains in third person**. The narrator can switch between different characters, but will stay doggedly with one until the end of a chapter or section.Sep 2, 2021

The ==third-person objective point of view== is the opposite of the third-person omniscient point of view. In third-person objective, the narrator doesn't have access to the thoughts and feelings of any character.
