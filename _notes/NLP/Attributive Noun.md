    <p>What is the difference between attributive noun and an adjective?</p>
  </li>
  <li>
    <p>An attributive noun and an adjective both serve to modify a noun, but they do so in slightly different ways:</p>
    <ol>1. <strong>Attributive Noun:</strong> An attributive noun is a noun that modifies another noun, usually indicating a quality or characteristic of that noun. In this usage, the attributive noun functions similarly to an adjective. For example, in the phrase "apple tree," "apple" is an attributive noun modifying "tree," indicating the type of tree.</ol>
    <br/>
    <ol>2. <strong>Adjective:</strong> An adjective is a word that directly modifies a noun, providing more information about its attributes or qualities. Adjectives can be used predicatively (after a linking verb, as in "The sky is blue") or attributively (before a noun, as in "blue sky"). Adjectives are more versatile and common in English than attributive nouns</ol>
    <p>The key difference between an attributive noun and an adjective is that an attributive noun is still a noun and retains its noun form while modifying another noun, whereas an adjective is a distinct word class specifically used to modify nouns.</p>
  </li>
</ul>
