# Mirror and Match

Brain synchronization, also known as brainwave entrainment or neural entrainment, is when brainwaves naturally align with external stimuli or other brains:
External stimuli
Brainwaves can synchronize to the rhythm of external stimuli like speech, music, flickering lights, or tactile stimuli.
Other brains
Brain waves can synchronize when people interact, such as when students and teachers work together or when people listen to a musical performance. This is called interbrain synchrony, and it can lead to better social and creative outcomes.
Learning
Brainwave synchronization may help with learning because it may precede changes in synapses, which are the connections between neurons.
Some ways to sync brainwaves include: Mirror and match, Active listening, Shared experience, and Emotional empath

### Electrons and Waves
- **Electrons as Particles and Waves**: In quantum mechanics, electrons are described as both particles and waves. This duality is known as the wave-particle duality. When an electron is observed, it can be detected as a particle, but its behavior (such as its position and momentum) is best described by a wave function.
- **Measurement of Electrons**: The "vibration in the field" you mentioned can be thought of as the wave function of the electron. This wave function describes the probability distribution of finding the electron at a particular location. When a measurement is made, the wave function "collapses" to a specific state, similar to how a sound wave can be measured at a specific point in space and time.

### Sound and Acoustics
- **Properties of Sound**: Sound waves are mechanical waves that travel through a medium (like air, water, or solids). They have properties such as frequency, wavelength, and amplitude, which determine their pitch, wavelength, and loudness, respectively.
- **Reverberation**: Reverberation is the persistence of sound after the original sound has stopped, due to the reflection of sound waves off surfaces. In an enclosed space, like a room or a skull, reverberation can significantly affect the sound environment.

### Acoustical Properties of the Skull
- **Skull as a Resonant Cavity**: The skull can act as a resonant cavity, meaning it can amplify certain frequencies of sound. This is why you might hear your own voice differently when you speak inside a small, enclosed space compared to an open environment.
- **Bone Conduction**: Sound can also be conducted through the bones of the skull. This is why you can hear your own voice and internal sounds more clearly when you plug your ears. Bone conduction is used in some hearing aids and headphones.

### Environment Outside Our Skin
- **External Acoustic Environment**: The environment outside our skin, including the air, buildings, and other objects, all play a role in how sound is transmitted and perceived. For example, sound waves can be absorbed, reflected, or diffracted by different materials, affecting the quality and clarity of the sound we hear.
- **Interaction with External Fields**: Just as sound waves interact with the environment, electromagnetic fields (which include the fields associated with electrons) also interact with the environment. These interactions can be complex and are studied in fields like electromagnetics and quantum electrodynamics.

### Connecting the Dots
- **Analogies Between Sound and Quantum Mechanics**: While sound waves and the wave functions of electrons are fundamentally different (sound waves are classical, while electron wave functions are quantum), there are some useful analogies. Both involve the concept of waves and the idea that these waves can be measured and analyzed.
- **Acoustics and Biology**: The acoustical properties of the skull and the external environment are important in understanding how we perceive sound. Similarly, the behavior of subatomic particles like electrons is crucial in understanding the fundamental nature of matter and energy.
