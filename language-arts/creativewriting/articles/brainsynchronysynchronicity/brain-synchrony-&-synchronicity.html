Brain Synchrony & Synchronicity

How these conceptual phrases are related

Brain synchrony and synchronicity are two distinct concepts that share a common thread of interconnectedness. Brain synchrony refers to the phenomenon where the neural oscillations, or brainwaves, of two or more individuals synchronize during interaction. This synchronization is believed to facilitate communication and understanding between individuals, playing a crucial role in social interaction.

On the other hand, synchronicity is a term used to describe events that seem to have no causal relationship but are perceived as meaningfully intertwined. The term has its roots in the Greek words syn-, meaning "together," and khronos, meaning "time." In essence, synchronicity refers to the simultaneous occurrence of events that appear significantly related but have no discernible causal connection.

The concept of synchronicity can be further understood through its various subtypes and their corresponding parts. For instance, simultaneity, a subtype of synchronicity, refers to the quality of events occurring at the same time, with co-occurrence and concurrency as its parts. Concomitance, another subtype, denotes the quality of happening at the same time as something else, with coincidence and coexistence as its parts. Coincidence, yet another subtype, is the temporal property of two things happening at the same time, with co-occurrence and concomitance as its parts.

While brain synchrony and synchronicity are distinct concepts, they intersect in the realm of interpersonal relationships. The synchronization of brainwaves during interaction may lead to experiences of synchronicity, where separate events are perceived as meaningfully intertwined. This correlation between brain synchrony and synchronicity, although not fully understood, suggests a fascinating interplay between our neurological processes and our perception of the world around us.

Synchroncity

Synchronicity is a term used to describe events that seem to have no cause but are meaningful coincidences. Synchronicity is when people interpret two separate experiences as being meaningfully intertwined, even though there is no evidence that one led to the other.

The Greek roots of the word are syn-, "together," and khronos, "time".

Carl Jung defined synchronicity as an acausal connecting principle, whereby internal, psychological events are linked to external world events by meaningful coincidences rather than causal chains. For example, if you think of someone and they call you the next minute, or if you dream of finding something precious and find it a few days later, these are examples of synchronicity.

Overall, these concepts are all related to the idea of time and how events relate to each other in time.

Brain Synchrony

In neuroscience, the pragmatic definition of "brain synchrony" refers to the coordinated activity of different parts of the brain, in which multiple regions of the brain are active at the same time and working together to perform a specific task or process information.

Neural activity refers to the electrical and chemical signals that allow different parts of the brain to communicate with each other. A neural network refers to the complex web of connections between neurons that allows for coordinated activity. A neuron is a single cell that transmits electrical signals in the brain, while a synapse is the junction between two neurons where signals are transmitted.

This definition serves to explain how different parts of the brain work together to support various cognitive processes, such as attention, perception, and memory.
